## Understanding the Myths Around Ad Block Tester Websites

Popular "ad blocker test" websites claim to reveal how well your content blocker is working by showing block counts or notifications. However, this guide will explain why these sites often mislead users and fail to reflect real-world browsing conditions.

### Myth 1: "A Higher Block Count Means a Better Content Blocker"

These test pages present a simple number—how many elements or ads they managed to block—and imply that a higher count indicates superior performance. This logic is flawed because:

- A high block count can mean your browser is actually downloading resources from more third-party servers, which contradicts the goal of reducing exposure.
- uBO Scope focuses on **unique third-party remote servers connected**, which is a better privacy indicator than simple block tallies.

### Myth 2: "Ad Block Tester Websites Show Reliable and Real-World Results"

These sites are often:
- **Unrealistic:** They use contrived network requests to servers that normal websites don’t use.
- **Unable to detect stealth blocking:** Many content blockers avoid revealing themselves by stealthily blocking or redirecting requests. These test pages cannot see the subtle reality.

As a result, their results may make a blocker appear less effective than it really is or vice versa.

<Tip>
Avoid relying on these test pages for judging content blocker quality. They do not represent actual web browsing ecosystem complexities.
</Tip>

## How uBO Scope Provides a Realistic Benchmark

uBO Scope operates differently from typical test pages:

- It passively **monitors all true network requests** your browser makes during real browsing.
- It categorizes these connections into **allowed**, **stealth-blocked**, and **blocked** domains.
- The extension reports the **number of distinct third-party servers contacted**, emphasizing real-world exposure.

This approach reveals what actually happens behind the scenes instead of relying on synthetic tests.

### What You See in uBO Scope's Popup and Badge:
- **Allowed:** Domains from which resources were successfully fetched.
- **Stealth-blocked:** Domains where requests were blocked silently (not detectable by webpages).
- **Blocked:** Domains where requests were blocked outright and errors occurred.

The toolbar badge shows the count of unique allowed third-party domains, guiding you to aim for fewer third-party connections for higher privacy.

## Practical Tips and Best Practices

- **Do not chase a high block count badge on test sites; aim for fewer unique third-party connections in uBO Scope.**
- **Understand stealth blocking:** Some content blockers block connections quietly without visible errors.
- **Use uBO Scope as a privacy audit tool during genuine browsing sessions rather than synthetic benchmarks.**
- **For filter list maintainers, uBO Scope reveals networks often seen in real use, enabling better, more actionable filter maintenance.**

<Warning>
Ad blocker test sites often include fake or irrelevant domains; do not use them to make privacy decisions.
</Warning>

## Troubleshooting Common Confusion

<AccordionGroup title="Troubleshooting Common Questions">
<Accordion title="Why Does uBO Scope Show Allowed Domains Even When I Have a Blocker Active?">
Many first-party or essential third-party servers need to be contacted for proper website functioning. uBO Scope correctly reports these allowed connections, helping you distinguish normal versus risky domains.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Why Are Ad Blocker Test Sites Showing Different Results From uBO Scope?">
Ad blocker test sites use unrealistic requests and cannot detect stealth blocking techniques. uBO Scope tracks actual browser network activity, giving accurate real-world insights.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="My Badge Count Sometimes Increases After Visiting Some Sites. Is That Bad?">
Yes and no. The badge shows how many unique third-party domains were connected during that tab. Some legitimate websites use more third parties (e.g., CDNs), so evaluate context rather than raw numbers.
</Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
