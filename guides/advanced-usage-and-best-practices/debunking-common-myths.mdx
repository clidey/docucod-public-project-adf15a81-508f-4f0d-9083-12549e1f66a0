# Debunking Common Myths in Content Blocking

uBO Scope provides a clear, objective window into the real network connections your browser makes, independent of content blockers or browser tricks. This guide dispels widespread misconceptions related to block counts, toolbar badge numbers, and unreliable "ad blocker test" pages. By understanding these myths, you will be empowered to interpret uBO Scope's data accurately and evaluate content blockers with clarity.

---

## 1. Understanding What uBO Scope Measures

Before diving into myths, it is essential to grasp what uBO Scope tracks and reports:

- **Toolbar Badge**: Shows the number of unique third-party remote servers your browser successfully connects to during page load. 
- **Popup Panel**: Details domains categorized by connection outcomes — "not blocked" (allowed), "stealth-blocked" (redirected), and "blocked" (failed or denied).
- **Network Requests**: Monitored through the browser's `webRequest` API, reporting request outcomes regardless of which content blocker is active.

<u>Key Insight:</u> uBO Scope focuses on actual network connections, not on raw block counts or webpage-detected behaviors.

---

## 2. Myth: "Higher Block Count Means a Better Content Blocker"

### Reality Check:

- A **higher block count** on a toolbar or extension icon does **not** indicate a better or more effective content blocker.
- Instead, what matters is the **number of distinct third-party remote servers your browser successfully connected to** — a **lower number is better** because it means fewer connections to potentially unwanted third parties.
- Sometimes, an extension that blocks *more* requests (higher block count) still allows connections to *more* unique third-party servers, potentially leaking more data.

### Why This Happens:
- Different blockers use diverse strategies; some silently allow certain connections or use stealth blocking which doesn't increment block counts as expected.
- Block counts reflect *attempted* blocking events, not final exposure to third parties.

### What to Do Instead:
- Use uBO Scope's badge count to evaluate real connection exposure.
- Check the popup's domain lists for allowed and stealth-blocked connections to understand actual network activity.

---

## 3. Myth: "Ad Blocker Test Pages Provide Reliable Results"

### Reality Check:

- **Ad blocker test pages are misleading and unreliable benchmarks.**
- They employ *fabricated, unrealistic network requests* to domains that no real site uses.
- Many content blockers implement stealth techniques that cannot be detected by scripts running on webpages, making such tests ineffective.

### Why Ad Blocker Test Pages Fail:
- They invite detection evasion tactics and do not reflect real-world browsing scenarios.
- They fail to measure actual exposure to third-party servers during normal browsing.

### Recommended Approach:
- Avoid these tests entirely for evaluating content blockers.
- Use uBO Scope to observe network requests from real websites to assess blocker effectiveness in practical situations.

---

## 4. How uBO Scope Provides Objective Measurement

uBO Scope leverages browser-level network monitoring:

- It records *all* network requests passing through the `webRequest` API.
- It categorizes requests into:
  - **Allowed**: Connections successfully established.
  - **Stealth-blocked**: Redirected to block but without direct failures.
  - **Blocked**: Requests that failed or were denied outright.
- The toolbar badge reflects **allowed** third-party unique domains, offering a direct metric of online exposure.

This approach bypasses the pitfalls of in-page detection and synthetic testing, giving users a transparent, real-time insight into network activity.

---

## 5. Practical Tips for Using uBO Scope to Assess Content Blocking

- Focus on the **badge count**: A lower number indicates less exposure.
- Use the **popup panel** to explore connection details, understanding which domains your browser is connecting to and how those connections are classified.
- Cross-reference your observations with real browsing sessions on resource-rich sites, not on test pages.
- Recognize that some third-party domains are legitimate (e.g., Content Delivery Networks) and a low, reasonable count is expected.

---

## 6. Common Pitfalls to Avoid

- Do not judge a content blocker by *block counts* shown in other extensions or test sites.
- Avoid referencing ad block test pages as a standard for blocker quality.
- Expect some third-party connections due to common web infrastructure; a zero count is almost never realistic.
- Understand that stealth blocking may hide some connections from page scripts but uBO Scope reports all from the browser network layer.

---

## 7. Example Scenario: Comparing Blockers Using uBO Scope

Suppose you use Content Blocker A and Content Blocker B:

- Content Blocker A shows a block count of 150, but uBO Scope's badge shows 35 unique third-party domains allowed.
- Content Blocker B shows a block count of 100, but uBO Scope's badge shows 50 unique domains allowed.

Despite lower blocks, Content Blocker A exposes fewer distinct third-party connections, making it objectively better at limiting network exposure.

## 9. Troubleshooting Common Questions

<AccordionGroup title="Common Questions About Myths and Measurement">
<Accordion title="Why does my blocker show fewer blocks but more connections?">
Because some blockers block fewer requests but allow connections to multiple distinct third-party domains. Block count alone doesn't reveal exposure.
</Accordion>
<Accordion title="Can stealth blocking fool uBO Scope?">
No. uBO Scope monitors browser network traffic beyond what page scripts can detect, ensuring stealth blocking is visible in the popup.
</Accordion>
<Accordion title="Why shouldn't I trust ad blocker test pages?">
They use artificial network requests and do not simulate realistic browsing, making them an unreliable benchmark.
</Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
